CAD Design & Reverse Engineering

Design Process

Autodesk Fusion 360

Overview

The Phones-R-Us project involved the design and development of two precision-engineered phone casings, each requiring a different CAD approach. The bottom casing was modelled directly from a fully defined client engineering drawing, while the top casing required reverse engineering from a physical component, introducing additional complexity due to incomplete and unclear dimensional data.

Autodesk Fusion 360 was used to create both detailed 2D drawings and accurate 3D CAD models. Where dimensions were missing or unclear, engineering judgement was applied by analysing proportions, cross-referencing multiple views, and deriving unknown values from known measurements.

Both final designs were exported as high-resolution STL files, ensuring compatibility with additive manufacturing processes and allowing the models to be directly used for 3D printing in later stages.

Top Casing
Reverse Engineered Drawing
Fusion 360

The top casing drawing was developed through reverse engineering of a physical component. Several dimensions were missing or unclear, particularly in cross-sectional views.

This challenge was addressed by cross-referencing multiple views and calculating missing values using known dimensions, ensuring consistency across the geometry.

Additional difficulty arose in identifying correct feature positions, which was resolved by analysing relative distances and reconstructing the design step by step in Fusion 360.

Bottom Casing
Client Drawing
Fusion 360

The bottom casing drawing was produced directly from a complete and clearly defined client engineering drawing.

All dimensions and references were provided, allowing for a structured and accurate CAD process with minimal ambiguity.

The primary focus was ensuring precise translation into Fusion 360 and verifying alignment between all features throughout the modelling process.

Top Casing

The 3D model was developed using reverse engineered data, requiring careful interpretation of geometry and continuous validation against the intended design.

A key challenge was modelling the varying depth and complex surfaces, which was resolved through incremental feature construction and repeated accuracy checks.

The final model was exported as an STL file, enabling direct use in 3D printing while preserving geometric accuracy.

The bottom casing model was created directly from the engineering drawing, allowing for a smooth and efficient modelling workflow.

All features were constructed based on clearly defined dimensions, with regular checks ensuring consistency with the original design.

The completed model was exported as an STL file, making it fully suitable for additive manufacturing processes.
